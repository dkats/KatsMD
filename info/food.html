Food

Early Childhood Feeding Development

When do I start introducing my baby to solid foods?

Your pediatrician will discuss starting solids with you at the 4-month visit. In general, the recommendation is to start introducing your baby to solid foods between 4-6 months of age.

When is my baby ready for solid foods?

  • Holds his head up well and sits with support
  • Shows interest in food, such as watching you eat, reaching for food and opening his mouth when he sees food
  • Starting to lose tongue thrust (seeing less pushing of food out of his mouth) when you feed him with a spoon
  • Can suck food off a spoon without gagging or pushing the food back out

Which foods do I start with?

There is no medical evidence that introducing solid foods in a certain order is better for babies. In the past, infant cereal was recommended as the very first solid food. While this will be an important part of your baby’s diet, it doesn’t have to be the very first food offered.

Common first pureed foods:

  • Rice cereal mixed with breast milk or formula
  • Baby foods made with meat
  • Vegetables: pureed sweet potato, squash, peas, spinach, carrots, green beans, avocado
  • Fruits: bananas, apples, pears, plums, nectarines, blueberries

After 4-6 months you can give:

  • Fish (well-mashed)
  • Eggs: well-mashed, scrambled, homemade french toast with egg, blended egg in fruit
  • Wheat (cereal/crackers that dissolve in mouth)
  • Peanut-containing products (or other tree nuts): thinned peanut butter (mixed with formula/breastmilk/water), peanut butter mixed into applesauce, chocolate pudding, or ice cream (if tolerating milk), smoothies, batters, sauces
  • Cheese and full-fat yogurt
  • Dairy: yogurt or shredded cheese
  • Soy: soy yogurt, tofu

Wait until 12 months before giving:

  • Honey
  • Cow’s milk

Do not give foods that your baby may choke on:

  • Hot dogs
  • Nuts
  • Whole grapes
  • Large meat chunks

Foods that cause harder stools:

  • Applesauce
  • Bananas
  • Rice
  • Dry cereal

Food that cause looser stools:

  • Pears
  • Plums
  • Prunes
  • Apricots
  • Water

How should I start?

Eating should be a fun and social event! Here are some tips to help your baby get off to a good start with trying solid foods:

  • Make sure your baby is in a high chair or sitting up straight to prevent choking. Pull the high chair or seat up close to the table.
  • Make it a point to have family meals from your baby’s first feeding.
  • Start new foods in the morning or afternoon so you can watch for any reactions. Aim for a time when your baby isn’t very sleepy or hungry. A good time is usually 1 hour after a bottle or nursing.
  • Serve small, bite-size portions, too much on the plate can make your child feel overwhelmed. Start with just 1-2 teaspoons of food and then increase the amount slowly over time. Try mixing the food with a little breast milk or formula to thin it out making it more “soupy” and easier to swallow.
  • Start with 1 new food every 2-3 days. Watch for new reactions, like a rash, diarrhea or vomiting. If your baby has a reaction to a new food, stop giving it to him and call the doctor.
  • Your baby might need to try a new food 10-15 times before he likes it. This is normal. If your baby doesn’t like a new food, try it again in a few weeks.
  • Feed your baby until he shows signs of being full. This includes shaking his head or pushing food away. Don’t keep trying to feed your baby if he doesn’t seem interested anymore.
  • After 9 months old, you will notice that your baby will shift to taking more solid food and less breast milk or formula. If possible, the American Academy of Pediatrics (which I agree with) recommends that you continue to breastfeed until at least 12 months.

Sensory hierarchy

If your child is having trouble handling new tastes and textures, you can try to progress along the sensory hierarchy for feeding:

  1. Look: Present your child with new solids to look at, and tolerate on his tray/plate. Praise him for looking at crackers, cookies, puffs, etc.
  2. Touch: Encourage your child to move crackers/cookies around on his plate/the table, and tolerate touching the solid. Have him place them in the middle of the plate, on the plate, off the plate, in bowls # 1, 2, 3, or stack several crackers (e.g., copy mom to pile 2 crackers on top of each other).
  3. Smell: Smell foods; “Smell the first cracker…what does it smell like?” (e.g., turn taking).
  4. Kiss/Lip Touches: Bring foods to lips for “kisses” or “lip touches.”
  5. Tongue Touches: Lick foods/snacks in their whole form.
  6. Bite and remove: Take a bite on “strong teeth” or molars and remove with hands or spit out.
  7. Bite and chew, and remove from the mouth.
  8. Bite and chew 3 times, move food to other side of mouth with tongue, chew 3 times, and remove.
  9. Bite, chew, and swallow.

Other tips:

  • Foods should be easy to chew, toddlers can't chew tough things
  • Toddlers like colorful foods
  • Try changing the venue of their meals: serve lunch in the playhouse, snack as an afternoon tea party
  • Toddlers enjoy playing with their food; it is a part of learning about it
  • Let them help in food preparation, take them to grocery store and give them specific choices ("Would you like carrots or broccoli?" "Should we get Cheerios or cornflakes?")
  • Grow a vegetable garden
  • Make food attractive, arrange food in the shape of an animal, a face, etc.
  • Eat as a family as much as possible; kids learn by imitating what they see
  • Help ensure that they come to the table hungry, don't use food to cure boredom or as a pacifier
  • It's ok if they don't eat much on a single day; what matters is that they're eating enough over the course of a week to grow well
  • Avoid forbidding foods or labeling them "special" or "treats"; if you don't want your child to eat something, keep it out of your home

Cooking and Nutrition

Tips for reading Nutrition Facts Label

Keep total calories in a day less than 2000.

Limit these nutrients:

  • Fat: Eating too much fat can increase your risk of diseases affecting every part of your body. You should limit the number of calories from fat to no more than 30% of your total daily calories and limit saturated fat calories to no more than 10% of the calories you take in each day.
  • Cholesterol: Too much cholesterol in your diet can also lead to heart disease. High levels of cholesterol are usually found in meat and dairy products.
  • Sodium: Too much sodium (salt) can contribute to high blood pressure and heart disease. Limit your sodium intake to no more than 1500 mg a day.
  • Sugar: Sugars are listed separately under carbohydrates to make it easier to track.

Get enough of these nutrients:

  • Dietary fiber: Dietary fiber will help keep your bowel movements regular and soft
  • Vitamins
  • How to Understand and Use the Nutrition Facts Label (U.S. Food and Drug Administration)

Shopping List: Foods to Choose and Foods to Limit

  • Drinks
    • Choose: Water, zero calorie drinks
    • Limit: soda, juice, milk
  • Dairy
    • Choose: yogurt, unprocessed or natural cheeses (e.g., Swiss, Gruyere, low-sodium)
    • Limit: buttermilk, cheese spreads, American, Parmesan, Romano, and Bleu cheeses
  • Meat, beans, eggs, and other proteins
    • Choose: fresh or frozen beef, chicken, lamb, veal, pork, turkey, fish, shellfish, eggs, peanut butter, dried beans and peas
    • Limit: ham, bacon, sausage, cold cuts, hot dogs, anchovies, sardines, salted cod, smoked herring, and corned beef
  • Fruits and vegetables
    • Choose: fruit (all fruit is low in sodium); fresh, frozen, and low-sodium canned vegetables; low-sodium tomato or vegetable juice
    • Limit: regular tomato or vegetable juice, sauerkraut, pickled beets, "boil-in-bag" vegetables, vegetables in sauces, regular canned vegetables, instant potatoes, and frozen dinners
  • Starches and snacks
    • Choose: plain and whole-grain breads and rolls, cereals, pasta, rice, and other homemade bread products, unsalted popcorn, unsalted snack foods, unsalted nuts
    • Limit: breads, rolls, crackers with salted tops, instant hot cereals, stuffing mixes, refrigerated biscuits, chips, pretzels, salted nuts, other salted snacks
  • Soups
    • Choose: homemade or low-sodium soups and stews; low-sodium bouillon cubes
    • Limit: canned, frozen, dried, or condensed soups; regular bouillon cubes
  • Seasonings, sauces, and condiments
    • Choose: herbs and spices such as sage, dry mustard, pepper, lemon, garlic powder, bay leaf, oregano, rosemary, thyme, vinegar, low-sodium soy sauce, salt substitutes (check with your doctor)
    • Limit: onion salt, garlic salt, sea salt, MSG, meat tenderizers, packaged and canned sauces or gravies, soy sauce, steak sauce, tamari, miso, olives, pickles, barbecue sauce

Portion Control and Mindful Eating

In a day:

  • ½ of your food should be fruits & vegetables
  • ¼ of your food should be grains (bread, pasta, potatoes, rice)
  • ¼ of your food should be protein (meat, chicken, fish, beans)

How can I control portions at home?

Take a standard serving out of the package and eat it off a plate instead of eating straight out of a large box or bag. This works well for snacks like potato chips.

Take seconds of vegetables or salads instead of higher-fat, higher-calorie parts of a meal such as meats or desserts.

When cooking in large batches, freeze food that you will not serve right away. This way, you won't be tempted to finish eating the whole batch before the food goes bad. And you'll have ready-made food for another day. Freeze in single-size meal containers.

Try to eat three sensible meals at regular times throughout the day. Skipping meals may lead you to eat larger portions of high-calorie, high-fat foods at your next meal or snack. Eat breakfast every day.

Plan your snacks. Eating too many snacks throughout the day may lead to weight gain.

When you do have a treat like chips, cookies, or ice cream, eat only one serving, eat it slowly, and enjoy it!

What is mindful eating?

Mindful eating means paying attention to what you eat. When you take time to enjoy your food, you are less likely to overeat. Here are some ideas to help you become more mindful:

  • Eat slowly so your brain can get the message that your stomach is full.
  • Stop eating when you feel full. Focus on enjoying the setting and your friends or family for the rest of the meal.
  • Avoid eating in front of the TV, in the car, or while you are busy with other activities.
  • Be careful about letting yourself get so busy that you forget to eat, get famished and just eat whatever you can grab. Take the time to eat regular meals throughout the day.
