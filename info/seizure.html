Seizures

Basic Seizure First Aid

  • Stay calm & track time; most seizures last a few minutes
  • If the seizure continues for longer than 5 minutes, call 911
  • Keep child safe: move any nearby objects out of the way
  • Do not restrain/hold down
  • Do not put anything in the childâ€™s mouth other than rescue medication prescribed by a physician
  • Administer emergency medicine prescribed by doctor (if applicable)
  • Stay with child until fully conscious
  • Do not give the person anything to eat or drink until they are fully alert
  • Video record seizure and/or write down a description of the seizure (paint a picture with your words)

For tonic-clonic (grand mal) seizure:

  • Protect head
  • Keep airway open/watch breathing
  • Turn child on side

A seizure is generally considered an emergency when:

  • A convulsive (tonic-clonic) seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes
  • There are repeated seizures without regaining consciousness
  • There is an injury
  • The person has diabetes
  • The person has breathing difficulties
  • The seizure is in water

Seizure Precautions/Safety

Usually these restrictions are maintained for at least 6 seizure-free months before consideration is given for lifting them. These restrictions are meant to protect your child and are based on the experiences of neurologists treating patients with epilepsy/seizures.

  • No swimming.
  • No baths. Your child should take showers. If your child is an infant or toddler and needs a bath, you should be with them at all times during the baths, without exception.
  • If your child is an infant or toddler, do not allow them close to any source of water—such as buckets, wading pools, and toilets—without accompaniment.
  • No climbing high places such as cliffs, high bar gymnastics, jungle gyms, etc. If your child is a toddler, he/she should not be allowed to climb a ladder to a slide unaccompanied.
  • No driving, skiing, snowboarding, rollerblading, or skateboarding. Riding a bicycle with a helmet is ok.
  • Regardless of your child's age, he/she should never be in a locked room at any time.
  • If your child has been 6 months seizure free, you can discuss with your neurologist about potentially lifting some or all of these restrictions.
