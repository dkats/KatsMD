Vitamin D

Infants

Why is vitamin D important?

It helps the body absorb calcium. Together, calcium and vitamin D help build strong, healthy bones and teeth.

It prevents diseases such as rickets.

It may also help prevent infections and diseases, such as multiple sclerosis and lupus, certain types of cancer, and diabetes.

What are sources of vitamin D for infants?

Sunlight exposure
Even though sunlight helps the body make some vitamin D, it is more important that infants stay out of direct sunlight and use protective clothing and sunscreen to prevent sunburn.

Foods
Breast milk is the ideal nutrition source for your baby through the first year of life. However, it does not have enough vitamin D (32 ounces of breast milk provides only 25 IU of vitamin D or less).
32 ounces of vitamin D-fortified milk or infant formula has 400 IU of vitamin D.
Some infant cereals have vitamin D added.

Does my child need a vitamin D supplement?

All breastfed infants should receive a vitamin D supplement (400 IU per day) beginning in the first few days of life.

All formula-fed infants taking less than 1 quart (32 ounces) of formula per day should receive a vitamin D supplement (400 IU per day).

Continue the supplement until your infant is getting 400 IU per day of vitamin D from his or her diet. If you are unsure, check with your pediatrician.

What kind of vitamin D supplements should I buy?

Infants should receive vitamin D in liquid form. Any supplement that contains 400 IU of vitamin D per dose is a good choice.

You can buy over-the-counter (no need for a prescription) supplements containing vitamin D alone (e.g., D-Vi-Sol®) or vitamin D combined with other vitamins and minerals (e.g., Tri-Vi-Sol®). Some places sell concentrated vitamin D drops (e.g., Carlson®). The drop can be applied to the nipple before latching or to a pacifier.

Most liquid forms of vitamin D contain 400 IU in each 1mL or 400 IU in each drop, depending on the brand. Read the instructions on the container to find out.
