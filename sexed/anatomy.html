Anatomy and Physiology (How the Body Works)

Body Parts

Male

Scrotum: The bag of skin that contains the testicles. It keeps them at the right temperature, which is slightly cooler than the rest of the body, by shrinking and expanding. If the temperature gets too warm, the sperm won't develop well. During puberty, the scrotum gets bigger/baggier and turns a slightly darker color.

Testicle/testis: (Plural: testicles/testes) The organ that makes sperm and testosterone. Usually hang at slightly different heights, which is totally normal.

Epididymis: Attaches to the back of each testicle and is where the sperm "learn" to swim and finish developing.

Vas deferens: (Pronounced "vaz DEF-er-enz") A tube that connects the epididymis (on each testicle) to the seminal vesicle. This is what carries sperm from the scrotum to inside the body (toward the penis) before ejaculation.

Seminal vesicle: Where sperm combine with fluid to make semen.

Prostate: Adds nutrients to semen

Urethra: Tube through which urine and semen leave the body (through the penis).

Penis: Organ that hangs outside the body and is used for urination and ejaculation. There is a wide range of shapes and sizes, which are determined by our genes and random chance. The size and shape have nothing to do with "manliness".

Female

Vulva: All of the external parts of the female sex organs (what you can see from the outside).

Labia majora: "Labia" is Latin for lip and "majora" is Latin for great, which in this context means large. The labia majora are the folds of skin between a female-bodied person's legs that cover the vaginal opening.

Labia minora: "Labia" is Latin for lip and "minora" is Latin for lesser. The labia minora are the folds of tissue between the labia majora that cover the vaginal opening. Going from outside inward, there are the legs, labia majora, labia minor, and then the vaginal opening.

Clitoris: A bump of tissue, about the size of a pea, where the labia minora meet in front. Inside it is a bundle of nerves that are very sensitive to touch, just like there are many nerves in the tip of the penis.

Urethra: The tube through which urine leaves the body (it connects the bladder to the outside world). It opens through a little hole between the clitoris and the vaginal opening. It is usually covered by the labia minora.

Vagina: A connection between the uterus and the outside world. This is where a penis would go during sex.

"G-spot": (Named after Dr. Gräfenburg) Some people have a very sensitive spot the inside of the vagina (towards the person's frontside). The "G-spot" isn't a different body part; it's actually part of the clitoris that gets stimulated from inside the vagina. Importantly, not everyone has this, because everyone's body is arranged slightly differently, especially when it comes to nerves.

Hymen: A thin piece of skin that may cover part of the opening. It is often small or missing and can break without sex. Unfortunately, checking for a hymen is frequently—and incorrectly—used to see if a female-bodied person is a virgin; however, this does not work! There is no test for virginity other than asking the person.

Cervix: Lower part of the uterus that forms a tight seal between the vagina and the rest of the uterus to keep the uterus sterile. It also makes mucus to keep sperm alive. The only things that can travel through the cervix are menstrual fluid (“blood”) and sperm. During labor, it opens and widens to let the baby come out. During sex, the penis cannotget through the cervix and only stays in the vagina.

Uterus: (Also known as the "womb") It's a huge pear-sized muscle and is where a developing baby (fetus) grows and is fed. It is also where “the period” comes from (we'll talk about this later).

Uterine tube: (Also known as a Fallopian tube) A tube between the uterus and an ovary (there are two ovaries, so there are two uterine tubes, one on the left and one on the right). This is where an egg cell meets the sperm to make a fetus.

Ovary: Where the egg cells (also called "ova") are stored and released. Also produces hormones, like estrogen.

Eggs and Sperm

Egg cells and sperm are called "sex cells" and are the cells that pass on your genes (DNA) to your children. Each egg cell and sperm have a completely unique mix of DNA, which is the instruction manual that babies use to grow and develop. They also only have half of the DNA that you need to make a baby, so when a sperm combines with an egg cell, a fully instruction manual is formed and a baby can develop.

How Are Sperm Released?

Sperm are stored in the epididymis. When it's time for them to be released (ejaculation), muscles squeeze them from the epididymis, through the vas deferens, and into the seminal vesicles. There, they combine with fluid to make semen, which travels through the urethra into and out of the penis. Usually, the penis is erect during this, which happens when the blood vessels in the penis cause increased blood flow. The blood fills the penis and makes it stiff, like how party balloon animals get inflated with air.

Where Do Sperm Go?

If ejaculated into a vagina, sperm travel up the vagina, through the cervix, into the uterus, and then into the uterine tubes, which is where they can fertilize an egg cell. The fluid ("mucus") made by the uterus, cervix, and vagina helps the sperm travel to the uterine tubes.

How Are Egg Cells Released? (Ovulation and Menstruation)

Girls are born with hundreds of thousands of tiny egg cells (also called "ova", pronounced OH-va; one is called an "ovum", pronounced OH-vum). In kids, these egg cells are only half-formed, At puberty, hormones tell the ovaries restart development of the ova and to slowly release them every month. Usually, one ovum develops and is released from an ovary every month, but sometimes two or none can be released. Ovulation is the scientific word for when an egg cell is released by the ovary. Even though there are two ovaries, only one of them is active (ovulates) each month (so, when sperm try to fertilize the egg cell, half of them go into the "wrong" ovarian tube, which is empty). The ovaries don't trade off which one releases an ovum every month, so it's not like your right will release in January, March, May, etc. and the left will release in February, April, June, etc. It's random (we can't predict which side will release on a given month), but the average is about half of the ova from one side and the other half from the other side.

Anyway, while an ovum develops in the ovary, the uterus starts to grow a thick lining on its inside wall. The lining is there to protect and feed a fertilized egg, which is an ovum that has combined with a sperm, in case it happens. The uterus has to be ready whether the egg is fertilized or not, because it takes about 2 weeks for the lining to grow enough to support a fetus. This cycle of developing an ovum and releasing it happens every month (until the ova run out).

If the egg is not fertilized, it won't stick to the wall of the uterus and will just flow out from the uterus into the vagina and out of the body. Then, the lining of the uterus, which has fully grown by this point, breaks down and also flows out of the uterus, through the vagina, and to the outside world. This is a "period" (also called a "menstrual cycle"). The bleeding lasts for about one week, stops, and then this cycle starts all over again, so the uterus starts building its lining again.

Pregnancy

When a sperm and an egg cell meet, they combine to form a fertilized egg. The fertilized egg then travels from the uterine tube into the uterus and implants into the uterine lining. When this happens, the female-bodied person becomes pregnant. The fertilized egg begins growing and developing and has many different stages. At first, it's called a zygote (z-EYE-goat), which becomes an embryo (EM-bree-yo), and the embryo develops into a fetus (FEE-tus).

Puberty

Puberty is when young bodies develop into adult bodies. There are many changes, but the most important ones are that people develop new feelings and interests and either their egg cells continue to develop or their sperm begin to be made. The egg cells develop while in the womb and pause their development until puberty when they continue. New egg cells are not made after birth. Sperm are not made at all until puberty, but they are constantly made from scratch (hundreds of thousands every day. This means that female-bodied people can run out of egg cells (which is called menopause), but male-bodied people will not run out of sperm. As males age, their sperm tend to have more and more problems, but there are usually enough well-functioning sperm to be able to get someone pregnant.

The changes in puberty are triggered by a hormone that is made in a part of the brain called the pituitary gland. This hormone travels through the blood to the testicles and ovaries, which then make other hormones, including testosterone and estrogen.

What's the Purpose of Sperm and Egg Cells?

DNA is like the instruction manual for our cells to function. Sperm and eggs each have one half of that instruction manual. When they combine, they make a baby with a full set of instructions.

Why Does Puberty Happen?

Hormones are chemicals that the body uses to send messages. The brain uses "sex hormones" to communicate with the "sex organs" (e.g., breasts, genitals) to tell them to develop. The main sex hormones are testosterone and estrogen. Both males and females make both, but in different amounts. Males make lots of testosterone, not so much estrogen. Females make lots of estrogen, not so much testosterone.

When Does Puberty Happen?

Puberty usually happens between 8- and 17-years-old. For female-bodied people, it usually starts between 10 and 13; for male-bodied people, usually between 12 and 14. That being said, there is huge amounts of variation and everyone's body is different. When you start makes no difference to how you develop; no age is better than any other one. Most of the changes happen in a few years, but can happen slowly over many years.

The important takeaway is that: No matter when or how you develop, IT IS NORMAL!!

Hygiene

Most of hygiene is pretty self-explanatory, but a topic that gets brought up frequently is washing the vagina ("douching"). Basically, you should not wash your vagina with anything other than water. Just water. Nothing else. At all. Even if you find soaps that say they're specially made for vaginas. Don't do it!

Why is this so important? The vagina is a very special and sensitive environment. Like in your gut, there are bacteria that are normally in the vagina. They keep it healthy and keep bad bacteria from growing there. If the bacteria get killed off or new bacteria are added, you can develop a disease like bacterial vaginosis ("BV") or vaginal candidiasis ("a yeast infection"). If you use a soap or any other product on your vagina, it will change that very sensitive environment by either killing the normal and healthy bacteria or adding new bacteria. The vagina is made to keep itself clean and healthy, so don't mess with the systems that are already in place. It's ok to use some water to rinse your vagina, but let your body do most of the work.

Sex

Types of Sex

  • Vaginal sex: penis-in-vagina intercourse
  • Oral sex: mouth-to-genital contact
  • Anal sex: penis-in-anus intercourse
  • Fingering or hand jobs: hand-to-genital contact
  • Dry humping: genital rubbing
  • Touching yourself: masturbation

All of these sexual activities, except masturbation, can carry the risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

Myths and Misconceptions

Masturbation

Masturbation (touching your genitals for pleasure) is completely 100% safe and a normal behavior. Some people like to masturbate and some people don't. The important thing to know is that either is perfectly ok. Masturbating is not cheating or a sign of something going wrong in a relationship; it's about discovering what feels good.

PMS (Pre-Menstrual Syndrome)

PMS is a real thing and affects different people in a variety of ways. It might affect mood, bloating, or general comfort levels. It happens in the two-week period between ovulation (when an egg cell is released by an ovary) and menstruation ("the period"). That being said, not all emotions are explained by PMS. If someone is upset about being broken up with or getting a bad grade on a test, please be a kind and supportive human, and don't just say, "Oh, she's just PMS-ing." That being said, there are options for helping to deal with PMS if it's interfering with your life, but that's a conversation between you and your doctor.

Toxins In the Uterus

This is a myth that comes up fairly often, but there are no toxins in the uterus! Your uterus and vagina do not need to be cleaned or detoxed (no matter what celebrities say). The uterus and vagina are clean environments that are specifically made to develop babies, so there is nothing harmful about what is inside them.

Facts and Questions

What Is Pre-ejaculate/Pre-cum?

After a penis becomes erect, but before ejaculation/orgasm, a small amount of fluid is pushed through the urethra to clean out any urine and make it a better space for sperm and semen to pass through. This fluid (just a few drops) is called pre-ejaculatory fluid ("pre-cum") and is made by a gland called Cowper's glands, which are right below the prostate. The fluid sometimes carries some sperm, but usually is not enough to make someone pregnant (however, there's still a chance, so don't take the risk!). It can carry sexually transmitted infections/diseases (STIs/STDs).

What Is "Squirting"/Female Ejaculation?

Internet videos make it seem like female-bodied people can ejaculate ("squirt"), when a lot of fluid seems to gush out of the vagina during orgasm. There aren't any glands or other ways for the body to make that much fluid all at once, and scientists have shown that if there's this much fluid coming out at once, it's actually coming from the bladder (urine), not the vagina (remember, the ureter empties through a little hole between the labia minora, right above the vagina). So, in these videos, they're either acting (or using special effects), or it's urine. That does not mean that it's wrong or shouldn't happen. If you or your partner has experienced it and finds it pleasurable, that's perfectly ok. There's nothing harmful about urine, and the whole point of sex/orgasm is to feel good.
